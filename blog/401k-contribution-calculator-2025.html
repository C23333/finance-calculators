How Much Should I Contribute to My 401(k) in 2026?

The IRS has updated 401(k) contribution limits for 2026. Here's exactly how much you should contribute based on your income, age, and financial goals.

2026 401(k) Contribution Limits

The IRS has increased 401(k) contribution limits for 2026:

Contribution Type 2026 Limit 2025 Limit
Employee Contribution (Under 50) $23,500 $23,000
Catch-Up (Age 50+) $7,500 $7,500
Total if 50+ (Employee Only) $31,000 $30,500
Total Limit (Employee + Employer) $70,000 $69,000

New for 2026: Super Catch-Up

If you're age 60-63, you can contribute an additional $11,250 (instead of $7,500), for a total employee contribution of $34,750.

The Minimum: Get Your Full Employer Match

At the very least, contribute enough to get your full employer match. This is free money - don't leave it on the table.

Common Employer Match Formulas:

  • 100% match up to 3%: Contribute at least 3% of salary
  • 50% match up to 6%: Contribute at least 6% of salary
  • 100% match up to 6%: Contribute at least 6% of salary
  • Dollar-for-dollar up to $X: Contribute at least $X

Example: $80,000 Salary with 50% Match up to 6%

Your Contribution Employer Match Total Annual
3% ($2,400) $1,200 $3,600
6% ($4,800) $2,400 (max) $7,200
10% ($8,000) $2,400 (max) $10,400

In this example, contributing 6% gets you the full match. Contributing more than 6% doesn't increase the match, but still grows tax-advantaged.

Recommended Contribution by Income Level

Entry Level ($40,000 - $60,000)

  • Minimum: Enough to get full employer match (typically 3-6%)
  • Target: 10-12% of salary
  • Priority: Build emergency fund first, then increase 401(k)

Mid-Career ($60,000 - $100,000)

  • Minimum: Full employer match
  • Target: 15% of salary (including employer match)
  • Strategy: Max out Roth IRA ($7,000), then increase 401(k)

High Earner ($100,000+)

  • Target: Max out 401(k) ($23,500)
  • Strategy: Also max Roth IRA if eligible, consider backdoor Roth
  • Bonus: Mega backdoor Roth if plan allows

The 15% Rule

Financial experts generally recommend saving 15% of your gross income for retirement, including employer match. Here's how that breaks down:

Salary 15% Target Monthly Amount
$50,000 $7,500/year $625/month
$75,000 $11,250/year $938/month
$100,000 $15,000/year $1,250/month
$150,000 $22,500/year $1,875/month

Traditional vs Roth 401(k)

Many employers now offer both options. Here's how to choose:

Choose Traditional 401(k) If:

  • You're in a high tax bracket now (24%+)
  • You expect lower income in retirement
  • You want to reduce current taxable income
  • You're close to retirement

Choose Roth 401(k) If:

  • You're in a lower tax bracket now (12-22%)
  • You expect higher income in retirement
  • You want tax-free withdrawals in retirement
  • You're young with decades of growth ahead

Pro Tip: Split Your Contributions

Consider splitting between Traditional and Roth 401(k) for tax diversification. This gives you flexibility in retirement to manage your tax bracket.

How to Increase Your Contribution

If you can't hit 15% right away, use these strategies:

1. Start with the Match

Begin by contributing enough to get your full employer match. This is an immediate 50-100% return on your money.

2. Increase by 1% Each Year

Bump your contribution by 1% annually. You'll barely notice the difference in your paycheck, but it adds up significantly over time.

3. Save Your Raises

When you get a raise, increase your 401(k) contribution by half the raise amount. You still get more take-home pay, but you're also saving more.

4. Use Windfalls

Tax refunds, bonuses, and gifts can boost your retirement savings. Consider increasing your contribution rate temporarily.

What If You Can't Afford 15%?

Don't let perfect be the enemy of good. Any amount helps:

  • 3%: Better than nothing, gets some employer match
  • 6%: Usually captures full employer match
  • 10%: Solid foundation for retirement
  • 15%: On track for comfortable retirement
  • 20%+: Early retirement possible

The Power of Starting Early

Time is your greatest asset. Here's how $500/month grows at 7% average return:

Starting at 25 instead of 35 more than doubles your retirement savings with the same monthly contribution.
