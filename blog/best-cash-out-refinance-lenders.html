What Is a Cash-Out Refinance and How Does It Work?

A cash-out refinance replaces your current mortgage with a new loan for more than you owe. You receive the difference as a lump sum at closing.

Example scenario: You owe $200,000 on a home worth $400,000. You have $200,000 in equity. With a cash-out refinance, you might take out a new loan for $280,000, pay off the original $200,000 balance, and receive $80,000 in cash (minus closing costs).

The new loan comes with its own interest rate, term, and monthly payment. Because you're borrowing more than before, your monthly payment will typically increase‚Äîeven if you secure a lower interest rate than your original mortgage.

Key mechanics to understand:

  • Loan-to-value (LTV) ratio: Most lenders cap cash-out refinances at around 80% LTV, meaning you must retain at least 20% equity after the transaction. Some lenders allow higher LTV ratios, but expect higher rates or mortgage insurance requirements.
  • Closing costs: Closing costs typically range from 2-5% of the loan amount and may be rolled into the loan or paid upfront.
  • New loan terms: You're starting a fresh mortgage, so a 30-year term means 30 more years of payments‚Äîeven if you were years into your previous loan.

How to Compare the Best Cash-Out Refinance Lenders

Not all lenders offer the same deal. Here's what to evaluate when shopping for the best cash-out refinance lenders:

Interest Rates

Cash-out refinance rates are typically slightly higher than standard refinance rates because lenders view them as riskier. Even a small rate difference compounds significantly over a 15- or 30-year loan.

What to do: Get quotes from at least three lenders on the same day (rates fluctuate daily). Compare the annual percentage rate (APR), which includes both the interest rate and fees, for a more accurate cost comparison.

Closing Costs and Fees

Lenders charge origination fees, appraisal fees, title insurance, and other closing costs. These can vary by thousands of dollars between lenders.

Common fees to compare:

  • Origination fees (often 0.5-1% of the loan amount)
  • Discount points (prepaid interest to lower your rate)
  • Third-party fees (appraisal, title, recording)

Some lenders advertise "no closing cost" refinances, but they typically compensate by charging a higher interest rate. Run the numbers to see which option costs less over your expected time in the home.

Loan-to-Value Requirements

Most conventional lenders require you to keep at least 20% equity after the cash-out. If your home is worth $400,000, you could borrow up to $320,000 total (including paying off your existing mortgage).

Some lenders offer higher LTV options‚Äîup to 85% or 90%‚Äîbut these come with trade-offs: higher rates, private mortgage insurance (PMI), or stricter qualification requirements.

Credit Score and Qualification Standards

Cash-out refinances typically require higher credit scores than purchase mortgages. Many conventional lenders look for a minimum score around 620, though you'll generally need a higher score for the most competitive rates.

Other qualification factors:

  • Debt-to-income (DTI) ratio requirements vary by lender
  • Stable employment and income documentation
  • Sufficient home equity
  • Property type (investment properties often face stricter requirements)

Customer Service and Process Speed

How quickly can the lender close? How responsive are they to questions? Online reviews and lender reputation matter, especially if you need funds by a specific deadline.

Cash-Out Refinance vs. Other Home Equity Options

A cash-out refinance isn't your only option for accessing home equity. Consider how it compares:

Home Equity Loan (Second Mortgage)

A home equity loan is a separate loan on top of your existing mortgage. You receive a lump sum and make fixed monthly payments.

Pros: Keeps your original mortgage intact (important if you have a low rate); fixed rate and payment.
Cons: Two separate monthly payments; potentially higher combined interest costs.

Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)

A HELOC works like a credit card secured by your home. You draw funds as needed during a set period, then repay.

Pros: Flexibility to borrow only what you need; interest-only payments during draw period.
Cons: Variable interest rates; payment can increase significantly during repayment period.

When Cash-Out Refinance Makes More Sense

  • Your current mortgage rate is higher than today's rates
  • You want to consolidate everything into one payment
  • You need a large lump sum rather than ongoing access to credit
  • You prefer the predictability of a fixed rate

When Other Options May Be Better

  • Your current mortgage rate is lower than available refinance rates
  • You only need a small amount of cash
  • You want to preserve your existing loan terms
  • You prefer flexibility over a lump sum

Common Uses for Cash-Out Refinance Funds

Lenders don't restrict how you use cash-out funds, but some uses make more financial sense than others.

Uses that may build value:

  • Home improvements: Renovations that increase your home's value can offset the cost of borrowing. Kitchen and bathroom remodels, energy efficiency upgrades, or adding square footage often provide returns.
  • High-interest debt consolidation: Replacing high-interest credit card debt with lower-rate mortgage debt can reduce total interest paid‚Äîbut only if you avoid running up new credit card balances.
  • Education expenses: Funding education can be a long-term investment, though compare with federal student loan options first.

Uses that carry more risk:

  • Lifestyle spending: Vacations, cars, or consumer goods depreciate. You'll be paying for them for decades.
  • Speculative investments: Using home equity to invest in volatile markets puts your home at risk.
  • Covering ongoing expenses: If you're borrowing to cover regular bills, address the underlying budget issue first.

The Cash-Out Refinance Process: What to Expect

Understanding the general timeline helps you plan:

1. Application
Submit your application with income documentation, asset statements, and information about your current mortgage.

2. Home Appraisal
The lender orders an appraisal to confirm your home's current value. This determines your maximum loan amount.

3. Underwriting
The lender verifies your information, reviews the appraisal, and makes a lending decision. They may request additional documentation.

4. Closing
You sign final documents, pay closing costs, and the new loan pays off your old mortgage. Cash-out funds typically arrive 3 business days after closing due to the federal right of rescission, which gives you 3 days to cancel.

Total timeline: Most cash-out refinances close within 30-45 days, though timing varies by lender and circumstances.

Key Takeaways

  • Shop multiple lenders and compare APRs, not just interest rates‚Äîfees vary significantly between lenders.
  • Most lenders require you to retain at least 20% equity after the cash-out, capping your loan at 80% LTV.
  • Cash-out refinancing works best when you need a large lump sum and current rates are favorable compared to your existing mortgage.
  • Use funds strategically for value-building purposes like home improvements or debt consolidation rather than depreciating purchases.
  • The federal right of rescission gives you 3 days to cancel after closing before funds are released.
