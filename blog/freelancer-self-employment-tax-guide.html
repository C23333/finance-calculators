Freelancer's Guide to Self-Employment Taxes

Self-employment taxes can be confusing for new freelancers. This guide explains exactly what you owe, when to pay, and how to reduce your tax bill legally.

What is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax is the Social Security and Medicare tax that self-employed people pay. When you're an employee, your employer pays half of these taxes. As a freelancer, you pay both halves.

Tax Employee Pays Self-Employed Pays
Social Security 6.2% 12.4%
Medicare 1.45% 2.9%
Total 7.65% 15.3%

Important

Self-employment tax is in addition to your regular income tax. A freelancer earning $100,000 might pay $15,300 in SE tax plus $15,000-$20,000 in income tax.

How to Calculate Self-Employment Tax

Here's the formula:

  1. Calculate net self-employment income (revenue - expenses)
  2. Multiply by 92.35% (this accounts for the employer-equivalent portion)
  3. Multiply by 15.3% for SE tax

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

Unlike employees who have taxes withheld from each paycheck, freelancers must pay estimated taxes quarterly.

2026 Quarterly Due Dates

Quarter Income Period Due Date
Q1 Jan 1 - Mar 31 April 15, 2026
Q2 Apr 1 - May 31 June 16, 2026
Q3 Jun 1 - Aug 31 September 15, 2026
Q4 Sep 1 - Dec 31 January 15, 2027

How Much to Pay Quarterly

To avoid penalties, pay at least:

  • 100% of last year's tax (110% if income over $150,000), OR
  • 90% of this year's tax

A simple approach: Set aside 25-30% of every payment you receive for taxes.

Tax Deductions for Freelancers

Reduce your taxable income with these common deductions:

Home Office Deduction

  • Simplified method: $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max)
  • Regular method: Actual expenses Ã— percentage of home used for business

Business Expenses

  • Computer and equipment
  • Software and subscriptions
  • Office supplies
  • Professional development and courses
  • Business travel
  • Marketing and advertising
  • Professional services (accountant, lawyer)

Health Insurance Deduction

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums for themselves and their family. This is an "above the line" deduction, meaning you get it even if you don't itemize.

Retirement Contributions

  • SEP IRA: Up to 25% of net SE income (max $70,000 in 2026)
  • Solo 401(k): Up to $70,000 total (2026)
  • Traditional IRA: Up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+)

The QBI Deduction

The Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction lets you deduct up to 20% of your net self-employment income. This can significantly reduce your income tax (not SE tax).

Retirement Options for Freelancers

Solo 401(k)

  • Best for: High earners who want to maximize contributions
  • 2025 Limit: $23,500 employee + 25% employer = up to $69,000
  • Pros: Highest contribution limits, Roth option available

SEP IRA

  • Best for: Simple setup, variable income
  • 2025 Limit: 25% of net SE income, up to $69,000
  • Pros: Easy to set up, flexible contributions

SIMPLE IRA

  • Best for: Freelancers with employees
  • 2025 Limit: $16,500 + 3% match
  • Pros: Lower admin costs than 401(k)

Common Freelancer Tax Mistakes

  1. Not paying quarterly - Results in penalties and interest
  2. Mixing personal and business expenses - Use separate accounts
  3. Not tracking mileage - Keep a log for business driving
  4. Missing deductions - Track every business expense
  5. Not saving enough - Set aside 25-30% of income
  6. Forgetting state taxes - Many states have income tax too

Tax Checklist for Freelancers

  1. Open a separate business bank account
  2. Track all income and expenses
  3. Save 25-30% of income for taxes
  4. Pay quarterly estimated taxes
  5. Maximize deductions (home office, health insurance, retirement)
  6. Consider hiring a tax professional
  7. Keep records for at least 7 years
