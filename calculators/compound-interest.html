Compound interest guide

Use this calculator to estimate how savings or investments grow with regular contributions. The projection assumes a constant rate and reinvested gains.

Time is powerful

Longer timelines allow interest to compound on itself, creating larger gains later.

Rate assumptions

Small differences in assumed return can lead to large differences in outcomes.

Consistent contributions

Adding money regularly often matters more than chasing short-term performance.

How compounding works

  • Interest is earned on both your original principal and previous interest.
  • More frequent compounding produces slightly higher totals.
  • Regular contributions increase the base that earns interest.

Real-world adjustments

  • Taxes, fees, and inflation can reduce real returns.
  • Markets fluctuate, so actual returns may be uneven year to year.
  • Revisit assumptions as your risk tolerance changes.

How to use the results

  • Compare different contribution levels and time horizons.
  • Use conservative rates for planning and upside cases for sensitivity.
  • Set milestones to track progress over time.

Compound interest calculator FAQ

Is the interest rate guaranteed?

No. The rate you enter is an assumption. Actual investment returns can vary each year and may be higher or lower than expected.

What compounding frequency should I use?

Use the frequency that matches your account terms. Many savings products compound monthly or daily, while some investments effectively compound as returns are reinvested.

Does this calculator include taxes or inflation?

No. The results are nominal and do not account for taxes, fees, or inflation.

How often should I contribute?

Consistent contributions are typically more important than frequency. Choose a schedule you can maintain, such as monthly or per paycheck.

About This Calculator

How to Use This Calculator

Enter your initial investment, monthly contribution, interest rate, and investment period to see how your money grows over time. Choose different compound frequencies to see how they affect your returns.

How Compound Interest Works

When your investment earns interest, that interest is added to your principal. In the next period, you earn interest on both your original principal AND the previously earned interest. This creates a snowball effect that accelerates your wealth building.

Key Factors That Affect Your Returns

  • Time: The longer you invest, the more powerful compounding becomes
  • Interest Rate: Higher rates lead to faster growth
  • Compound Frequency: More frequent compounding yields slightly higher returns
  • Regular Contributions: Adding money consistently dramatically increases your final balance

The Rule of 72

A quick way to estimate how long it takes to double your money: divide 72 by your interest rate. This is a simple approximation for planning.
