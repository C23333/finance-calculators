Self-Employment Tax Guide

What is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax is the Social Security and Medicare tax for people who work for themselves. When you're an employee, your employer pays half of these taxes. When you're self-employed, you pay both halves - a total of 15.3%.

How SE Tax is Calculated

  1. Calculate net self-employment income (gross income minus business expenses)
  2. Multiply by 92.35% (this accounts for the employer-equivalent portion)
  3. Apply 12.4% Social Security tax (up to $168,600 wage base)
  4. Apply 2.9% Medicare tax (no limit)
  5. Add 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax if over $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married)

Reducing Your Tax Burden

  • Track all business expenses: Every legitimate deduction reduces your taxable income
  • Contribute to retirement: SEP-IRA allows up to 25% of net self-employment income
  • Deduct health insurance: Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums
  • Home office deduction: If you use part of your home exclusively for business
  • Deduct half of SE tax: You can deduct the employer-equivalent portion from your income tax

Estimated Tax Payments

Self-employed individuals must make quarterly estimated tax payments if they expect to owe $1,000 or more in taxes. Failing to make these payments can result in penalties. Use Form 1040-ES to calculate and pay your estimated taxes.

Record Keeping

Keep detailed records of all income and expenses. Save receipts, invoices, and bank statements. Good record keeping makes tax time easier and protects you in case of an audit.
