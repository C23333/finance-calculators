Self-employment tax planning guide

This calculator estimates SE tax, federal tax, and a simplified state tax to help you plan quarterly payments. Use it as a starting point and verify current rules.

Net earnings

Start with gross income minus business expenses to estimate taxable net earnings.

Tax layers

SE tax covers Social Security and Medicare, then income taxes apply on top.

Quarterly payments

Estimated taxes are paid through quarterly due dates throughout the year.

What this calculator includes

  • SE tax on 92.35% of net earnings, including the wage base cap.
  • Additional Medicare tax above the threshold for your filing status.
  • Simplified federal brackets and state tax estimates.
  • Deductions for half of SE tax, retirement contributions, and health insurance premiums.

What it does not include

  • Itemized deductions, tax credits, or Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction.
  • Local taxes, self-employment surtaxes, or state-specific brackets.
  • Phaseouts, alternative minimum tax, or special case rules.

Ways to improve accuracy

  • Update assumptions for the latest IRS wage base and brackets.
  • Use your actual state tax rules instead of the simplified estimate.
  • Account for credits and deductions you regularly claim.

Self-employment tax calculator FAQ

What is self-employment tax?

Self-employment tax covers Social Security and Medicare for people who work for themselves. You pay both the employer and employee portions.

When are quarterly payments due?

Estimated tax payments are generally due April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15. Dates can shift for weekends or holidays.

Can I reduce my SE tax?

You can reduce taxable income through eligible business deductions, retirement contributions, and health insurance premiums. You can also deduct half of SE tax from income tax.

How accurate is this calculator?

This is a simplified estimate and does not cover every rule or deduction. Use IRS guidance or a tax professional for precise calculations.

How to Use This Calculator

Enter your gross self-employment income and business expenses. Select your filing status and state. Add any other income sources, retirement contributions, and health insurance premiums. The calculator will compute your SE tax, federal and state income tax, and estimated quarterly payments.

Self-Employment Tax Basics

  • SE Tax Rate: 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare)
  • Applied to: 92.35% of net earnings
  • SS Wage Base: Updates annually based on SSA guidance
  • Additional Medicare: Applies above thresholds that vary by filing status

Key Deductions

  • 50% SE Tax: Deductible from income tax
  • Health Insurance: 100% deductible for self-employed
  • Retirement: SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) limits depend on IRS caps and net earnings
  • Home Office: If used exclusively for business
  • Business Expenses: Supplies, equipment, travel, etc.

How SE Tax is Calculated

  1. Calculate net self-employment income (gross minus expenses)
  2. Multiply by 92.35% (employer-equivalent adjustment)
  3. Apply 12.4% Social Security tax (up to the annual wage base)
  4. Apply 2.9% Medicare tax (no limit)
  5. Add 0.9% Additional Medicare if over threshold

Frequently Asked Questions

What is self-employment tax?

Self-employment tax is the Social Security and Medicare tax for people who work for themselves. When you're an employee, your employer pays half. When self-employed, you pay both halves - a total of 15.3%.

When are quarterly payments due?

Quarterly estimated tax payments are generally due April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 of the following year. Dates can shift for weekends or holidays.

Can I reduce my SE tax?

You can reduce taxable income through business deductions, retirement contributions (SEP-IRA, Solo 401k), and health insurance premiums. You can also deduct half of your SE tax from income tax.
